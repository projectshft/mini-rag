If You’re Over 30 and Trying to Learn to Code, Read This First.

When’s the last time you really learned something?

Not a quick YouTube tutorial. Not a how-to guide. I’m talking about deep, uncomfortable, make-you-question-how-smart-you-are type of learning. The kind that rewires your brain.

For most people, it hasn’t happened since school and that’s part of the problem.

There’s a reason the traditional path to software engineering involves school.

Structured learning, homework and feedback loops train your brain to endure confusion, repetition, boredom, and struggle. But when you’re over 30, trying to switch careers with a job, a family, and limited time… that structure is gone.

You’ve probably developed some skills and are pretty good at a few things.

You’re not used to sucking at something new so your tolerance for it is low.

15 Years Ago, My Life Was Unrecognizable

I wasn’t sitting behind a laptop writing JavaScript. I was addicted to drugs, drinking heavily, and paying rent through illegal means. Coding wasn’t on my radar. A W-2 wasn’t on my radar.

Fast forward seven years: I was flying first class to New York to give a talk in front of a room full of Fortune 500 executives. No CS degree. No Ivy League resume. Just years of self-learning, some luck, and a lot of building.

I don’t know many fields that let you completely reinvent your life. Tech does. But don’t confuse that opportunity with ease. This was not easy. And it’s definitely not a get-rich-quick scheme.

People join a coding bootcamp, memorize a few things, and expect job offers to rain from the sky in 90 days. When they don’t, they scream “Scam!”

Let me tell you what the real scam is: thinking you can memorize information and get paid six figures for it.

This is not a memorization job. It’s a thinking job. A curious person’s job. And more than anything, it’s a job that rewards the people who learn how to learn.

If you’re over 30, thinking of breaking into tech, here are 4 mindset shifts you’re going to need to make to have a shot at success.

1. Learn How to Learn (Seriously)

You’re not in school anymore. No one is chasing you. You have to own your learning process or you will get eaten alive.

You need to:

Filter resources instead of binging every tutorial

Actually read the documentation

Ask better questions, especially when stuck

Practice spaced repetition and revisit concepts over time

Write down what you don’t understand, not just what you do

If you want a method, here’s a good one:

The Feynman Technique

Pick a topic (e.g. closures, promises, the call stack)

Try to explain it in simple terms

Identify what you can’t explain

Go back, learn it again, then re-explain it

That’s the core of how people at Parsity get good. But we take it one step further:

2. Learn by Teaching (on Camera)

The most uncomfortable (and most effective) way to learn? Teach.

At Parsity, we have students do something that makes 99% of people squirm:

Learn a concept, then record themselves teaching it.

No ChatGPT. No script. Just freestyle, off the dome.

Teach what a closure is.

Explain how a debugger works.

Walk through asynchronous JavaScript to a non-technical audience.

Then go back and watch yourself. Cringe if you must. Then do it again. And again.

Do this 100 times, and I guarantee the fear disappears. The only reason people fear speaking or teaching is because it’s novel. Do it enough, and it becomes boring — like anything else.

I say this as someone who used to hate speaking in meetings. Now? I’ve made close to a thousand videos. The nerves go away. The clarity improves. And your understanding becomes stronger.

If you can teach it, you know it.

3. Develop Curiosity or Don’t Bother

Curiosity isn’t optional, it’s a silent requirement for this job.

You have to be the kind of person who wonders: How does that work? Why does that break? What happens if I change this?

This isn’t a checklist-following job. It’s a tinkerer’s job. You’re an engineer in the same way a mechanic is: breaking things, rebuilding them, and understanding how they tick.

If you’re not curious about what’s happening under the hood, it’s going to be a grind. But if you lean into it, coding becomes less like homework and more like solving puzzles for a living.

4. Build It. Break It. Rebuild It.

Stop trying to memorize everything.

Clone apps. Break them. Rebuild them. Forget the people who say clone projects are worthless they’re wrong.

I hate to say I’m guilty of this 😬. Check out John Crickett’s Coding Challenges for some examples of complex software to replicate.

Every time you rebuild something from scratch, you internalize the architecture. You recognize patterns. You finally get why something works not just that it does.

Imitation is the beginning of mastery.

Final Thought

If you’re over 30, switching careers, and trying to learn to code, you’re not too late. But you are on a different path. You’re bringing more baggage, more responsibility, more real-world experience and less free time.

That’s not a disadvantage. It’s your edge IF you develop the mindset.

Learn how to learn.

Teach to understand.

Stay curious.

Build more.
