# Junior Developers: How to Not Suck at Your Software Engineer Interview

---

Junior Developers: How to Not Suck at Your Software Engineer Interview

The company I work for is an exciting period of growth. Our engineering team is likely to double before the year’s end and we recently did around 20 interviews for 2 open junior roles on our team. These roles, like most you will see nowadays, require Javascript and more specifically ReactJS.

Nearly all of the interviewees went to a bootcamp which taught React, Javascript and typically NodeJS and MongoDB. The MERN stack seemed to be a popular stack at bootcamps across the board. After doing around a dozen interviews, some patterns really started to emerge in the process.

HTML and CSS — what’s that?

Bootcamps have a short amount of time to cover the basics of web development, teach you a framework, databases, some algorithms and how to deploy your app among other things. They’ve collectively decided that HTML and CSS were the most dispensable things they could eschew it seems.

I was consistently surprised (shocked?) that candidates struggled or plain didn’t know the difference between classes and ids. Some general concepts/features I would expect to receive for an entry level position would include:

FlexBox

Box Model

IDs/classes

HTML5 features

Pseudo-elements

Can You Explain…

One of the most common pitfalls our junior interviewees made during the interview was not being able to explain a concept without relying on screen sharing. While I understand that it’s often easier to show someone rather than explain — it can appear to indicate a lack of understanding.

If we ask how would you explain promises to someone who doesn’t know what they are and you show us a working implementation of a promise, it doesn’t necessarily mean you know much about it beyond how to use it.

Learning to speak about abstract concepts can be difficult. Reading articles about topics which confuse you and writing notes explaining, in plain English, things like closure, promises and asynchronous programming will get you closer to true understanding.

As a rule of thumb, if you can’t explain a concept to a 5 year old, you probably don’t know it that well.

Javascript

Our interview centered primarily on Javascript knowledge since that is the language we use for both our micro-services and front-ends. JS has a low barrier for entry, as far as learning is concerned which has advantages and uhhh, not so advantages. Nearly anyone can quickly crank out some poorly written Javascript but as interviewers we want to test whether you have the fundamentals down or just a cursory understanding.

The Javascript ecosystem is so large and encompassing that there’s no way we could reasonably expect you to know all the quirks and obscure details of the language but when an interviewee says they have never used any of the many ES6 features such as map, reduce, filter or knows the difference between const and let it certainly raises some concerns.

I’ve been through more than a few Javascript interviews and here are the questions I’ve been asked the most:

Difference between apply, bind, call

Closure — what is it and how would you use it?

Event delegation

What is the event loop?

Difference between map and forEach

What are some of the benefits of functional programming?

Difference between const and let

Prototypical inheritance vs class inheritance (for Juniors, this is kind of a stretch question — be prepared to explain the prototype chain)

What is this

This is by no means an exhaustive list, but these questions are so common I still rely on them for brush-up material.

While interviewers (well, us at least) may not expect you to have an in depth answer for many of these questions, it’s always impressive when a candidate can do a deeper dive into one of these concepts… without screen sharing hopefully.

Any Questions for Us

Most of the candidates we interviewed had thoughtful questions about what work is like, aspects of our job we like/don’t like and the on-boarding process.

Many of these conversations were insightful and allowed the interviewee to learn more about us and the job.

Some other candidates seemed wholly unprepared to ask any questions beyond what are the next steps. A smaller number did not seem to know anything about our company and asked questions that showed they did about zero research before walking into that zoom call.

If you are a junior on the job hunt, you likely are juggling a lot plates — studying, LinkedIn, writing and re-writing your resume and applying for every job you find on the internets. I would only advise you do some minimal research on the company you are interviewing and your interviewers. Check out their LinkedIn profiles and see if you can gain any insight into them and what they’ve worked on. Maybe they’ve written an article like this 😉.
