200 15 Minute Conversations with Software Developers from around the World — What I learned Surprised Me

Earlier this year I decided to start a business coaching software developers. One problem — I had no clue how to start a business.

I tried promoting on Instagram… crickets.

Paid ads on Facebook — 👎

Google Ads banned me before I could start… wtf

I paid thousands for coaches to show me path to monetization which led to more Facebook Ads… 😑

Then I decided to try LinkedIn. It seemed like a natural fit to reach my customer base: junior developers looking to pass interviews and increase their technical depth.

I was scared though.

What would my co-workers think? Would more developed developers pick me apart? Who would take me seriously?

I did it anyways. I’m nothing if not a stubborn bastard.

I wrote Monday — Friday with moderate success and attention. I gained a few customers. Then one day I saw my phone going crazy with notifications.

Holy sweet fish cakes.

Kyle Simpson reposted me! My followers skyrocketed and then I noticed my calendar began to fill with appointments…

Like a lot of them.

Honestly, few of these calls translated to any sales. I’m fine with that and I still do these calls to give back to the developer community. I have no expectation of any compensation during these meetings and I’ve learned so much that I find them very valuable.

I’ve spoken with developers from the US, India, Pakistan, Nigeria, Europe, Australia, Canada and plenty of other countries.

200 meetings later, here’s what I learned:

100% of developers feel impostor syndrome

This honestly shocked me.

I would speak with founders of tech startups. Senior developers at top tech companies and of course a ton of bootcamp grads. The most recurring theme in these conversations was the feeling that we just don’t know as much as our co-workers or others in the industry at our level.

One particular call stood out to me. The meeting was with a senior software engineer at Amazon who had been recently promoted.

What could I possibly offer him in the way of advice?

He felt like luck had simply been on his side throughout his journey and he would be “found out” at any moment. He had around 6 years of experience.

I know this feeling all too well. I’m a self-taught developer with a super non-traditional background. I was basically a street level hustler before learning to code (another story, another day).

What’s worked for me and what I recommend to others is to list out the skills you feel are missing from your tool belt and make an actionable plan to achieve those skills.

Check out this worksheet here to get clear on your goals and take steps towards beating impostor syndrome.

Most of your LinkedIn profiles and resumes have the same problems

Top issues:

Painting yourself as “junior”, “aspiring” or “learning” — no one really wants to hire a junior… let them figure out your level once you interview

Irrelevant job history… no work history? Make a site for your cousin and deploy it. Now you’re a freelance developer. You’re welcome.

Few connections on LinkedIn. Get to 500.

Limiting yourself more than you need to. Apply for jobs where you meet around 50% of the requirements. Shave 1–2 years off the desired YOE.

Grab this LinkedIn Cheatsheet.

Developers want to learn new skills but don’t know how… or what to learn

There’s a lot to learn.

Too much.

My generic advice? Put away the tutorial for a bit. Stop watching YouTube videos.

Build something outside of work that uses technology you are interested in or want to increase your skill with. This project does NOT need to be a public affair. It can be janky. It can look less than polished. The point is to learn. This is how you make yourself more hire-able and actually gain skills that will translate into practical use at your current job and the next one.

Grab a step by step guide to creating a great side project.

New grads are overly-focused on DSA and toy problems

There are 301,626 open positions for software engineers on LinkedIn at the time of writing this.

There are a handful of companies that make up the letters in FAANG.

Don’t bet the house on cracking the insane interview circuit at these companies and don’t limit your study plan to LeetCode.

I’m also not saying you shouldn’t study LeetCode. Be smart about it though:

Study concepts over specific problems

Learn common approaches

Code data structures from scratch to learn how to leverage them

Read Alex Xu’s book on system design

The rest of your studying should be related to the tools of your trade. If you’re a JS developer, you’ll need to know language specific features, how to use the most popular frameworks and prepare for pair-programming sessions to show off what you know.

I recommend using pramp.com for live practice with another human and checking out my study guide for DSA.

We could all use a boost

Life is rough. No matter who you are or where you’re from.

Rejection sucks.

Feeling under-appreciated at work is draining.

Family problems affect all of us.

Sometimes these conversations would be me just listening. I get it. We all need to unload sometimes and I’m humbled and honored that people have trusted me to share their personal issues and insecurities.

Be optimistic. I don’t care if that sounds cheesy.

We could all use some more cheese in our life.

Understand that none of us have it all figured out. I have gained some knowledge of a very small slice of life and I’m happy to share what’s worked for me, in the hopes it can help you on your own journey.

Looking forward to the next 200 calls.
