I Spoke to 643 Software Developers: Here are 3 Issues ALL Developers Struggle With and How to Tackle Them

Over the last 2 years, I’ve spoken with 643 developers from every corner of the planet. 160 hours of yapping on the phone with many of you reading this.

These were short, 15 minute conversations meant to tackle your most pressing issues as a developer. Sometimes these conversations lead to mentorship services or enrollment to Parsity but that’s never the goal.

I continue to learn a ton from these conversations which help me in my own business and career. Also — I see giving back as a duty from all the help I’ve received over the years.

I understand the major pain points in the community. I hear what’s working and what’s not working. The successes, failures and most importantly — the patterns.

Your problems are not unique. At all.

The kid in Pakistan from a family of engineers and doctors who got a computer science degree is struggling with the same issues as the woman in California who graduated from a bootcamp after working as a cashier.

Ok, so you’re dealing with the same problems. Now what?

You’re a software engineer. You want solutions not sympathy. I have a bit of both for you 😅.

Here are the top 3 problems that nearly every developer struggles with and some material to help you overcome it.

1. Impostor Syndrome: The Silent Battle

“I feel like the worst developer on the team”

“I’m not ready to apply yet”

“I just don’t get this stuff”

We all know this feeling and often, there is some truth to our feelings. I have been the worst on a team. It’s not fun.

Here’s what I’ve done and what I recommend you do:

Write out the skills you are missing from your toolbox. What do the “smart” developers know and do that you do not?

Make a plan to acquire these skills.

Prove to yourself that you deserve the position that you’re in.

Check out this doc here to get started.

Also — just know that I’ve yet to speak to a person that felt “ready” for anything.

Confidence comes AFTER you start, not before.

2. The First Job: A Daunting Mountain to Climb

I know, I know. You’ve applied to every company in the Tri-State area.

No one is hiring.

The market is saturated.

I’ve heard some version of all these frustrations for nearly 10 years. In fact, the ONLY time I stopped hearing these phrases was during the pandemic hiring spree.

Some harsh truths:

Mass applying does work — I don’t care what influencer tells you differently

Networking works too

Luck is a factor no one wants to admit

You can control your skills — not the timeline to get hired

Some stuff you should fix quickly:

Get 500 connections on LinkedIn to be more discoverable

Remove any mention of junior/aspiring/student from your profiles

Don’t apply for only junior roles — let the market decide your title

Use strong language on your resume to stand out

Check out this guide here to fix your resume.

3. Choosing Projects: The Art of Decision Making

“What should I build?”

Depends.

First off — forget the portfolio. Barely anyone will ever look at it. The purpose of a side project is 2-fold:

Create some emotional attachment to your learning so you don’t quit

Improve your RELEVANT practical skills

Most bootcamp grads and university students find that their productivity drops off a cliff after graduating. Without structured learning, they rely on random tutorials or get sold on another language to learn.

It’s hard to sustain interest in your craft when you’re pounding LeetCode problems or infinitely watching YouTube videos.

Having a side project you actually care about will naturally keep you interested and involved in your education.

Don’t stray too far from your stack at first either. If you are a recent grad, your project should involve mostly technology you are familiar with and maybe 1 or 2 new things you’d like to add to your tool belt. I strongly suggest TypeScript and unit testing since 98.5% of coding programs don’t touch these topics.

Parsity does. I mean, of course.

Now What?

I hope you find some comfort in knowing that you are not alone in your struggles. I was pretty shocked to find out just how common these problems are. Against advice from my own mentors, I plan to continue these calls, free of charge.
