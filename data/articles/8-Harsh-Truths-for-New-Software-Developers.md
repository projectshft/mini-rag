# 8 Harsh Truths for New Software Developers

---

8 Harsh Truths for New Software Developers

The software engineering market didn’t die. It just moved on without telling you.

We’re in a new era.

The bootcamp-to-job pipeline is dried up.

Certificates are noise.

AI tossed a grenade into the hiring funnel.

There are more software jobs open now than there were two years ago AND it’s more difficult to get hired.

It feels like the rules are completely different because they are.

Here’s some advice that’s too harsh for LinkedIn but I think you’ll find useful if you’re a newer developer on the market.

1. Vibe coding is not a career or a replacement for actual skill

Copy/pasting from ChatGPT and “figuring it out later” isn’t a real workflow.

Build less like a hacker, more like an engineer.

You should understand your tools, the stack, and what happens after you click “deploy.”

Vibe coders are running up cloud credits by exposing private information or scrambling to hire back software developers the moment their app grows beyond simple use cases or a few dozen files.

I give it another year and “vibe coders” will be regarded with the same reverence as “prompt engineers.”

Remember when that was a thing?

2. No one cares about your certificate.

Your Coursera badge?

That AI Fundamentals YouTube playlist?

The completion certificate from Parsity.io? (btw — I run this program and still don’t advise mentees to share this… just saying)

Hiring managers aren’t impressed and it can actually be a red flag, unless you’re in DevOps or some role that requires AWS/GCP certifications. For any full stack role, these certs are more noise than signal.

Certs don’t necessarily hurt but they rarely help.

4. If you must build a portfolio, don’t do it from scratch.

Use Vercel’s V0 or another AI site builder.

This weekend, I vibe-coded an entire site that looks better than anything I could’ve done on my own. Check it out.

Should that scare me?

I don’t know.

I do know that building fancy landing pages is not a job I want to do.

AI tools will almost certainly make something better than your hand-coded portfolio that breaks as soon as I put it in mobile view.

Let’s be honest: when digging through a stack of resumes, no one is going to look at your links. Following random links is a big no-no at most orgs and managers simply don’t have time.

Yes — I’ve met more than a few devs who got hired because of their portfolio. In 2013.

5. Full-stack is the new baseline.

“Front-end only” roles are fading.

Stack Overflow’s yearly survey reported only 10% of professional developers identify as front-end.

I was shocked and a little sad to see this but it tracks with my experience.

Modern teams expect you to be able to touch the backend, databases, and maybe even cloud infra.

You don’t need to be an expert in all these areas. You just need to be fluent enough to be deployed to these areas.

Start with Node + Express and here’s a free starter kit to get going:

👉 https://github.com/CodeCoachJS/node_express_starter

6. AI productivity is a lie — but you still have to play the game.

There’s a sickening trend of devs claiming they’re “10x more productive” because of AI.

Companies want to believe this.

Their shareholders are betting on it.

What could go wrong?

Google found out that AI improved developer productivity by 10%.

Not 10x. 10%.

But why ruin the fun with facts and numbers?

So what do you do?

Become “AI-first” — I hate myself a little for even typing that…

On the shallow end:

Use Cursor or Claude or whatever

Mention it on your resume

Auto-complete better and faster

Experiment with agents and different models/tools for software development

Managers are scrambling to hire AI-enabled developers to appease their bosses who told their investors they would become 100x more productive this year by becoming “AI-native” — whatever the fuck that means.

Now, if you actually want to develop the hard skills related to LLMs?

Learn RAG

Read up on transformers, attention mechanisms and paged attention

Use vector databases

Build agentic workflows

These are the skills that will actually make you valuable and I cover all of it in the upcoming Parsity AI Developer Course for people who want to build real, modern, AI-powered software.

7. Learning four languages makes you look unfocused.

Pick one stack and go deep.

JavaScript + TypeScript is enough.

Listing Rust, Go, Python, and Java after six months?

🚩

While I’m at it: remove those proficiency levels under the languages you list. No one knows what 65% at TypeScript means and it scares recruiters.

8. You’ll never feel “ready.”

There’s no magical “I’m ready now” moment.

Apply before you’re ready.

If you meet 50% of the requirements for a role then you need to apply.

If you meet 100% of the requirements then you’re over-qualified.

Don’t down-level yourself. The titles junior/mid-level/senior mean completely different things at different organizations.

Let the interview dictate your ability to do the job.

Stop filtering yourself out before anyone else does.

9. Hiring is broken but your network isn’t.

Yes, I know some people are still mass applying and getting interviews.

But at Parsity, the people who land jobs are doing something else:

They’re using their real-world network and it works.

They’re former teachers, chefs, EMTs.

They find their first job by reaching out to former colleagues, old classmates, friends-of-friends, or local businesses.

It’s rarely someone you know directly that can help you with that first role. It’s almost always a friend of friend of a friend.

Be methodical and do a BFS of your network.

If you don’t get that reference… look it up.

Bottom line

This market isn’t hopeless. It’s different.

It rewards deep skills, real projects, product thinking, and practical AI fluency (or at least the illusion of it 😅).

My super opinionated advice:

If you’re an early career developer and lacking in technical skills: consider a strategic investment in up-skilling with AI by building out a full stack web app using agents or RAG.

If you are having zero luck in the job market — consider doing an audit of your network to “shake the tree” and find some people who might be able to point you to someone you can talk to.

As usual, I hope that’s helpful and not too harsh.
