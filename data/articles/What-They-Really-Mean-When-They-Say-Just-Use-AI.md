# What They Really Mean When They Say “Just Use AI”

---

What They Really Mean When They Say “Just Use AI”

When people first started talking about AI in tech, the promise felt exciting: we have a new tool to push the boundaries of what’s possible.

But what it’s turned into is something very different.

Today, when someone tells you to “just use AI,” what they really mean is:

Do it faster.

Not better. Not smarter. Not safer.

Just faster.

And if the quality is worse? Who cares.

The Hidden Cost of AI

Let’s make no mistake here: the average quality of what AI produces is worse than what humans produce.

One study observed that it slows down senior software engineers by up to 20%. GitHub itself has reported increased bug rates in AI-assisted code.

But the uncomfortable truth is that nobody really cares.

Companies don’t care about quality if they can shave a few hours off a sprint. Investors don’t care if the codebase is brittle as long as the demo works before the next round.

And junior developers — many of whom I mentor at Parsity — don’t always realize that relying on AI too heavily is actively hurting their careers.

I’ve seen stories of juniors getting fired because they couldn’t actually write code beyond prompting. Remember when we scolded devs for copying and pasting from Stack Overflow?

Cursor and Claude basically do this at scale. Like a hyped-up intern snacking on Adderral who grinds LeetCode for fun.

The Path of Least Resistance

Here’s the sneaky trap: as developers (and humans), our natural instinct is to take the easiest path.

Why wrestle with a hard problem when you can just throw it at AI?

Why tell your boss something will take three days when you can say, “I’ll use AI, it should only take a couple hours”?

The problem is that easy path leads somewhere ugly: a mountain of brittle, unmaintainable AI-generated code that you’ll eventually have to answer for.

Unless, of course, your plan is to jump ship every three months before the fallout hits.

A New Flavor of Snake Oil

This dynamic has created a new class of snake oil salesmen.

I’ve met them.

They waltz into companies promising to “build this in record time with AI.” CEOs eat it up. Who doesn’t want faster AND cheaper?

They don’t care about building anything that lasts.

They only need to get the company to the next investment round, or to a sellable milestone. After that, the mess is someone else’s problem.

Meanwhile, they’ve left behind systems that are fragile, insecure, and dangerous for actual users. Look no further than Reddit threads full of developers who lost credentials or exposed sensitive data because someone decided to vibe code with no clue of basic software practices… or common sense.

The Skill That Matters Now

A lot of people like to make the lazy claim that “coding is going to be obsolete.”

That’s not just wrong, it’s foolish and shows a fundamental lack of understanding of what developers actually do.

Newsflash — it’s more than writing code and slamming coffees.

The role of a developer is changing. But the skill that will matter most isn’t just how well you can code — it’s how well you can push back.

Knowing when to say, “No, we can’t use AI for this.”

Knowing how to explain the limits of our AI tools

Knowing when enough is enough

That ability to resist pressure, to defend quality, and to protect the long-term health of the product is going to separate the professionals from the prompt jockeys.

Push Back, Smartly

I’ve spent the last year working with AI tools myself, watching how they’ve been pushed top-down inside companies, and mentoring developers who are trying to navigate this new landscape.

And my conclusion is simple: your job isn’t to blindly “just use AI.”

Your job is to know when not to.
