# I Used to Suck at Coding Interviews. Then I Quadrupled My Salary.

---

I Used to Suck at Coding Interviews. Then I Quadrupled My Salary.

Five years ago, during an interview for a senior dev role, I had a panic attack.

My heart was pounding.

I couldn’t get my words out.

I had to ask the interviewer to restart the call.

I was embarrassed, shaky, and 100% sure I’d blown it.

Plot twist: I got the job, but it was one of many interview disasters I’ve had over the years. And it showed me just how broken my approach was.

Here’s the thing: I was already decent at writing code. I wasn’t struggling to do the job. I was struggling to get the job. That’s a really frustrating place to be.

In one interview at the beginning of my career, a guy asked me about JavaScript object referential equality. I fumbled hard. He ended up explaining it to me during the call, like I was a student — not a candidate.

Another time I bombed a Facebook interview so badly that I just cut it short. I couldn’t solve a recursion problem and I knew I was just wasting time so I pulled the plug and bailed early.

I left these interviews thinking:

Maybe I’m just not cut out for this.

But I’m stubborn. And obsessed with patterns. So I started breaking the whole process down and rebuilding it from the ground up.

Here’s what changed everything:

1. I Built a Library of Personal Stories

I’ve done somewhere around 100 interviews in the last 11 years as an engineering manager and software engineer. I’ve noticed that most interviews have the same 4–5 behavioral questions, just reworded:

Tell me about a time you solved a hard technical problem

Tell me about a time you had a conflict with a teammate

What’s a project you’re proud of?

When did you fail?

So I started cataloging real stories from my work that mapped to those questions.

I’d jot them down in a doc, grouped by theme. I knew the stories cold — what happened, what I learned, and what they revealed about me.

As an engineering manager, I asked these same questions and what I realized I was really trying to understand was:

“Can I imagine this person working here?”

Once I understood that, I shaped my own stories to “sell” myself.

What good is passing the coding round in an interview just to bomb the behavioral sections? These questions will often make up the majority of your interview rounds and yet they get the least attention online.

2. I Got Clear on the Technical Gaps

I didn’t try to become a master of everything. I focused on the concepts that tripped me up repeatedly: closures, prototype inheritance, recursion and coding challenges.

I built small projects in vanilla JavaScript. I made videos explaining these concepts out loud. I wrote code until I didn’t need to look things up anymore.

Eventually, I compiled all of it into a repo I now share with the devs I mentor and it often kicks their ass.

3. I Trained Myself to Be Calm

This was the hardest part.

I’m naturally a nervous, high-energy person. So I did something weird: I sought out interviews I knew I’d fail. Just to get used to the feeling.

I used Pramp. I interviewed with strangers. I bombed questions. I embarrassed myself. But I got used to it.

After a while, interviews started to feel routine. Like skydiving (at least, I imaging): terrifying the first time, boring after 100 jumps.

I wanted interviews to be boring.

And eventually… they were.

The Result?

My salary went from $60K to a peak of $290K, with a $200K base.

Obviously, the salary bumps changed my life, but more importantly, I’m a lot less worried about IF I can land another role.

Learning to interview is the single most high leverage skill you can have as a software developer.

Is it fair? No

Is it reality. F*ck yes it is.
